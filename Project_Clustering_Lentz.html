KMeans¶

from sklearn.cluster import KMeans
from scipy.spatial.distance import cdist
clusters=range(1,10)
meanDistortions=[]

for k in clusters:
    model=KMeans(n_clusters=k)
    model.fit(data2Scaled)
    prediction=model.predict(data2Scaled)
    meanDistortions.append(sum(np.min(cdist(data2Scaled, model.cluster_centers_, 'euclidean'), axis=1)) / data2Scaled.shape[0])


plt.plot(clusters, meanDistortions, 'bx-')
plt.xlabel('k')
plt.ylabel('Average distortion')
plt.title('Selecting k with the Elbow Method')
Out[26]:
Text(0.5, 1.0, 'Selecting k with the Elbow Method')

Elbow analysis:¶

Thankfully its fairly clear to see in the plot above that we should choose either 3 or 4 but that after 4 there is little to gain by increasing the number of clusters. What this plot shows is which amount of clusters will give us the least average distance between all the points assigned to that cluster and the centroid. This is a way of testing how well a model performed on unsupervised learning technicques since we do not have a label to actually grade them on.

6-7. Compare models¶

Silhouette scores should be close to 1 in order to consider it the best fit. A number approaching 0 indicates that there is significant overlap and that the clustering is not that helpful in displaying, explaining or grouping the data points. A number approaching the negatives means the point is assigned to the wrong cluster so hopefully we don't get a model that bad at assigning points.

The models above didn't do awful but they didn't do that great. There was a bit of overlapping even though we chose to go with 4 clusters for all the models based on the elbow graph we saw earlier. The model that probably did the best based on the silhouette score and other metrics used above is the "ward" Hierarchical Clustering model.

You can visually verify the overlap yourself if you look closely at the boxplots and compare across the groupings. When there is overlap for many values for many columns, its hard for the model to determine which group it belongs to or makes more sense to put in, therefore, the less overlap we have the better.

In terms of understanding the two different linkage options that were chosen:

  • ’ward’ uses the Ward variance minimization algorithm. With ward I used 'distance' as the criterion for fcluster to choose the clusters. This means that the original observations in each flat cluster have no greater a cophenetic distance than t.
  • 'weighted', aka WPGMA, is the proximity between two clusters as the arithmetic mean of all the proximities between the objects of one and the objects of the other. With this one I used 'maxclust' as the criterion for fclusters in order to choose a cluster for each point. This means the criteria is to find a minimum threshold r so that the cophenetic distance between any two original observations in the same flat cluster is no more than r and no more than t flat clusters are formed

"Ward" seemed to behave much better as it did a better job of deciding on a smaller set of clusters on its own. You can see above that I left max_d at 50 and it was still able to decide on 3 max clusters necessary. However, "weighted", did not do as well. I had to set max_d to 4 and enforce that only 4 clusters were made. If I left that number at 50, it was creating all 50 clusters and assigning the points to them which is probably not a good number of clusters for such a small dataset which would be a red flag and something to investigate.
